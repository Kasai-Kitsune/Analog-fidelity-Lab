Why CRTs Looked Like This

Electron beam scanning

A single beam sweeps left-to-right, top-to-bottom at ~15.7kHz (NTSC). Only the spot under the beam glows at any instant — we see scanlines because of the Gaussian beam profile and gap between lines.

Phosphor triads & mask

Color CRTs use red/green/blue phosphor dots or stripes behind a metal mask. Sony Trinitron used vertical aperture grille wires; shadow-mask tubes used dotted triads (~0.28mm pitch).

Limited bandwidth

Composite video shared luma and chroma on one wire. The 3.58MHz NTSC color subcarrier caused dot crawl (checkerboards) and chroma was low-resolution, causing color bleed.

Interlaced scanning

480i transmitted 240 lines at 60 fields per second (odd then even). This saved bandwidth but created flicker and twitter on fine details.

Persistence & bloom

Phosphors like P22 decay in ~1-2ms (medium persistence). Electrons scatter in glass, creating halation — a soft glow around bright areas. P31 green phosphor used in oscilloscopes persisted much longer.

CRT Audio — The Sound of Scanning

CRTs produced audible sound! The horizontal flyback transformer vibrated at ~15.7kHz due to magnetostriction. Its amplitude modulated with picture brightness — bright scenes made it louder. Power supply ripple at 60/120Hz created a low hum. VHS tapes added wow & flutter — slow pitch wobble from motor drift and fast flutter from capstan imperfections.
